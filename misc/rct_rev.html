RCT

Under the Rubin causal model, the causal effect on unit ii is: Δi=Yi(1)−Yi(0)\Delta_i = Y_i(1) - Y_i(0) and the sample average treatment effect (SATE) is Δ‾=1n∑i=1nYi(1)−Yi(0)\bar{\Delta}=\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n Y_i(1) - Y_i(0) where the corresponding population average treatment effect (ATE) is

τ=𝔼[Yi(1)−Yi(0)]\tau = \mathbb{E}[Y_i(1) - Y_i(0)]

Both causal estimands SATE and ATE are unobservable.

In a randomized controlled trial one (perhaps the most intuitive) way to estimate the causal estimands is the difference-in-means estimator:

τ̂DM=1n1∑Di=1Yi(1)−1n0∑Di=0Yi(0) \hat{\tau}_{DM} = \frac{1}{n_1}\sum_{D_i=1} Y_i(1) - \frac{1}{n_0}\sum_{D_i=0}Y_i(0)

With this estimator we make the assumption that the potential outcomes model is valid – is also called the stable unit treatment value assumption (SUTVA): Yi=Yi(Di),∀iY_i = Y_i(D_i),\,\forall i

Then, if the treatment is randomized, all units are treated with the same probability, conditional on the potential outcomes and the number treated

ℙ[Di=1∣{Yi(0),Yi(1)}i=1n,n1]=n1n,i=1,…,n \mathbb{P}[D_i = 1 \mid \{Y_i(0), Y_i(1)\}_{i=1}^n, n_1] = \frac{n_1}{n}, \quad i = 1, \ldots, n

and τ̂DM\hat{\tau}_{DM} is finite-sample unbiased:

𝔼[τ̂DM|{Yi(0),Yi(1)}i=1n,n0>0,n1>0]=Δ‾ \mathbb{E}[\hat{\tau}_{DM} | \{Y_i(0), Y_i(1)\}_{i=1}^n, n_0 > 0, n_1 > 0] = \bar{\Delta}

To show this, consider just the first sum of the τ̂DM\hat{\tau}_{DM}, where

𝔼[1n1∑Di=1Yi|{Yi(0),Yi(1)}i=1n,n1]=𝔼[1n1∑i=1nDiYi|{Yi(0),Yi(1)}i=1n,n1](expand to n terms)=𝔼[1n1∑i=1nDiYi(1)|{Yi(0),Yi(1)}i=1n,n1](SUTVA)==1n1∑i=1nYi(1)𝔼[Di∣{Yi(0),Yi(1)}i=1n,n1](chain rule)=1n∑i=1nYi(1)(random assignment) \begin{align*} & \mathbb{E}\left[\frac{1}{n_{1}}\sum_{D_i=1}Y_{i}\Big|\{Y_{i}(0),Y_{i}(1)\}_{i=1}^{n},n_{1}\right]\\ & =\mathbb{E}\left[\frac{1}{n_{1}}\sum_{i=1}^{n}D_iY_{i}\Big|\{Y_{i}(0),Y_{i}(1)\}_{i=1}^{n},n_{1}\right]\quad\text{(expand to n terms)}\\ & =\mathbb{E}\left[\frac{1}{n_{1}}\sum_{i=1}^{n}D_iY_{i}(1)\Big|\{Y_{i}(0),Y_{i}(1)\}_{i=1}^{n},n_{1}\right]\quad\text{(SUTVA)}\\ & ==\frac{1}{n_{1}}\sum_{i=1}^{n}Y_{i}(1)\mathbb{E}[D_i\mid\{Y_{i}(0),Y_{i}(1)\}_{i=1}^{n},n_{1}]\quad\text{(chain rule)}\\ & =\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}Y_{i}(1)\quad\text{(random assignment)} \end{align*} an analagous result holds when n0>0n_0>0.

Now, assuming the treatment randomization is described by a Bernoulli trial, i.e.:

Di|{Yi(0),Yi(1)}∼iidBernoulli(π),0<π<1D_i | \{Y_i(0), Y_i(1)\} \stackrel{iid}{\sim} \text{Bernoulli}(\pi), \quad 0 < \pi < 1

we have the plug-in variance calculation for the difference in means estimator (since n0/nn_0/n and n1/nn_1/n estimate 1−π1-\pi and π\pi respectively):

V̂DM:=nn02∑Di=0(Yi−1n0∑Di=0Yi)2+nn12∑Di=1(Yi−1n1∑Di=1Yi)2 \hat{V}_{DM} := \frac{n}{n_0^2} \sum_{D_i=0} \left(Y_i - \frac{1}{n_0} \sum_{D_i=0} Y_i\right)^2 + \frac{n}{n_1^2} \sum_{D_i=1} \left(Y_i - \frac{1}{n_1} \sum_{D_i=1} Y_i\right)^2

and, without proof, we write n(τ̂DM−τ)⇒N(0,VDM),VDM=Var[Yi(0)]1−π+Var[Yi(1)]π\sqrt{n}(\hat{\tau}_{DM} - \tau) \Rightarrow N(0, V_{DM}), \quad V_{DM} = \frac{\text{Var}[Y_i(0)]}{1-\pi} + \frac{\text{Var}[Y_i(1)]}{\pi}.

Regression Adjustment(s)

There are two standard ways to estimate treatment effects via linear regression. The first is to fit a simple linear regression: Yi∼α+Diτ+Xi⋅βY_i \sim \alpha + D_i\tau + X_i \cdot \beta and report the resulting coefficient τ̂SREG:=τ̂\hat\tau_{SREG}:=\hat{\tau} as an estimate of the treatment effect.

The second is to add treatment-covariate interactions, fit the interacted linear regression:

Yi∼α+Diτ+Xi⋅β+DiXi⋅γ Y_i \sim \alpha + D_i\tau + X_i \cdot \beta + D_iX_i \cdot \gamma

and report the interacted regression estimator:

τ̂IREG=1n∑i=1nα̂+τ̂+Xi⋅(β̂+γ̂)−1n∑i=1nα̂+Xi⋅β̂=τ̂+X‾⋅γ̂ \begin{align*} \hat{\tau}_{IREG} & =\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}\hat{\alpha}+\hat{\tau}+X_{i}\cdot(\hat{\beta}+\hat{\gamma})-\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}\hat{\alpha}+X_{i}\cdot\hat{\beta}\\ & =\hat{\tau}+\bar{X}\cdot\hat{\gamma} \end{align*} where: X‾:=1n∑i=1nXi\bar{X} := \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n X_i

However, the simple regression assumes that the effect of covariates XiX_i is the same in both treatment and control groups. If this assumption is wrong, the estimator can be biased. The interacted regression includes the interaction term DiXi⋅γD_i X_i \cdot \gamma which makes it robust to differences in how covariates affect outcomes across treatment groups.

In randomized trials, we want our estimator to be unbiased even if our regression model is completely wrong. The interacted regression estimator:

  • Is always consistent for the ATE (even with model misspecification)
  • Never performs worse than difference-in-means asymptotically
  • Often performs strictly better than difference-in-means

The interacted regression is equivalent to stratification when covariates are discrete:

τ̂IREG=∑xnxnτ̂(x)\hat{\tau}{IREG} = \sum_x \frac{n_x}{n} \hat{\tau}(x) where τ̂(x)\hat{\tau}(x) is the difference-in-means within each stratum xx. This connects it to the well-established practice of stratified analysis.

Linear Model for Potential Outcomes

We can understand the interacted regression, as separate regressions for observed treated and control groups:

Yi(w)=α(w)+Xi⋅β(w)+εi(w) Y_i(w) = \alpha^{(w)} + X_i \cdot \beta^{(w)} + \varepsilon_i^{(w)}

with

𝔼[εi(d)|Xi]=0,Var[εi(d)|Xi]=σ2 \mathbb{E}[\varepsilon_i^{(d)} | X_i] = 0, \quad \text{Var}[\varepsilon_i^{(d)} | X_i] =\sigma^2

In this case we have the following observables relationship:

Yi=α(0)+Di(α(1)−α(0))+Xi⋅β(0)+DiXi⋅(β(1)−β(0))+εi Y_i = \alpha^{(0)} + D_i(\alpha^{(1)} - \alpha^{(0)}) + X_i \cdot \beta^{(0)} + D_iX_i \cdot (\beta^{(1)} - \beta^{(0)}) + \varepsilon_i

Variance Analysis Under Linearity

Difference-in-means variance (with π=0.5\pi= 0.5): VDM=4σ2+∥β(0)+β(1)∥A2+∥β(0)−β(1)∥A2V_{DM} = 4\sigma^2 + \|\beta^{(0)} + \beta^{(1)}\|_A^2 + \|\beta^{(0)} - \beta^{(1)}\|_A^2

where ∥v∥A2=v⊤Av\|v\|_A^2 = v^\top Av for convenience.

Asymptotic behavior for regression coefficients:

nw((α̂(w)β̂(w))−(α(w)β(w)))⇒N(0,σ2(100A−1)) \sqrt{n_w} \left(\begin{pmatrix} \hat{\alpha}^{(w)} \\ \hat{\beta}^{(w)} \end{pmatrix} - \begin{pmatrix} \alpha^{(w)} \\ \beta^{(w)} \end{pmatrix}\right) \Rightarrow N\left(0, \sigma^2 \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & A^{-1} \end{pmatrix}\right)

Central limit theorem for interacted regression: n(τ̂IREG−τ)⇒N(0,VIREG),VIREG=4σ2+∥β(0)−β(1)∥A2\sqrt{n}(\hat{\tau}_{IREG} - \tau) \Rightarrow N(0, V_{IREG}), \quad V_{IREG} = 4\sigma^2 + \|\beta^{(0)} - \beta^{(1)}\|_A^2

Variance comparison: VIREG=VDM−∥β(0)+β(1)∥A2≤VDMV_{IREG} = V_{DM} - \|\beta^{(0)} + \beta^{(1)}\|_A^2 \leq V_{DM}

General Theory Without Linearity

Conditional mean and variance functions: μ(w)(x)=𝔼[Yi(w)|Xi=x],σ(w)2(x)=Var[Yi(w)|Xi=x]\mu^{(w)}(x) = \mathbb{E}[Y_i^{(w)} | X_i = x], \quad \sigma^{(w)2}(x) = \text{Var}[Y_i^{(w)} | X_i = x]

Best linear projection coefficients: (α*(w),β*(w))=argminα,β𝔼[(Yi(w)−α−Xi⋅β)2](\alpha_*^{(w)}, \beta_*^{(w)}) = \arg\min_{\alpha, \beta} \mathbb{E}[(Y_i^{(w)} - \alpha - X_i \cdot \beta)^2]

General Central Limit Theorem

n(τ̂IREG−τ)⇒N(0,VIREG)\sqrt{n}(\hat{\tau}_{IREG} - \tau) \Rightarrow N(0, V_{IREG})

where:

VIREG=Var[Xi⋅(β*(1)−β*(0))]+1π𝔼[Yi(1)−α*(1)−Xi⋅β*(1)]2+11−π𝔼[Yi(0)−α*(0)−Xi⋅β*(0)]2 V_{IREG} = \text{Var}[X_i \cdot (\beta_*^{(1)} - \beta_*^{(0)})] + \frac{1}{\pi}\mathbb{E}[Y_i^{(1)} - \alpha_*^{(1)} - X_i \cdot \beta_*^{(1)}]^2 + \frac{1}{1-\pi}\mathbb{E}[Y_i^{(0)} - \alpha_*^{(0)} - X_i \cdot \beta_*^{(0)}]^2

Mean squared error of best linear predictor: MSE*(w)=𝔼[Yi(w)−Xiβ*(w)−α̂*(w)]2\text{MSE}_*^{(w)} = \mathbb{E}[Y_i^{(w)} - X_i\beta_*^{(w)} - \hat{\alpha}_*^{(w)}]^2

For balanced RCTs with constant noise (π=0.5\pi = 0.5, σ(1)2(x)=σ(0)2(x)=σ2\sigma^{(1)2}(x) = \sigma^{(0)2}(x) = \sigma^2): VIREG=VDM−∥β*(0)+β*(1)∥A2V_{IREG} = V_{DM} - \|\beta_*^{(0)} + \beta_*^{(1)}\|_A^2

Regression as Average Difference in Predictions

we have τ̂IREG=1n∑i=1nμ̂(1)(Xi)−1n∑i=1nμ̂(0)(Xi)\hat{\tau}_{IREG} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n \hat{\mu}^{(1)}(X_i) - \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n \hat{\mu}^{(0)}(X_i)

where μ̂(w)(x)\hat{\mu}^{(w)}(x) denotes linear regression predictions at xx under treatment dd.

  • μ̂(0)(Xi)=α̂(0)+Xiβ̂(0)\hat{\mu}^{(0)}(X_i)=\hat{\alpha}^{(0)} + X_i\hat{\beta}^{(0)} (predicted outcome under control)

  • μ̂(1)(Xi)=α̂(1)+Xiβ̂(1)\hat{\mu}^{(1)}(X_i)=\hat{\alpha}^{(1)} + X_i\hat{\beta}^{(1)} (predicted outcome under treatment)

τ̂IREG=1n∑i=1n[(α̂(1)+Xiβ̂(1))⏟μ̂(1)(Xi)−(α̂(0)+Xiβ̂(0))⏟μ̂(0)(Xi)] \hat{\tau}_{IREG} = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n \left[\underbrace{(\hat{\alpha}^{(1)} + X_i\hat{\beta}^{(1)})}_{\hat{\mu}^{(1)}(X_i)} - \underbrace{(\hat{\alpha}^{(0)} + X_i\hat{\beta}^{(0)})}_{\hat{\mu}^{(0)}(X_i)}\right]
